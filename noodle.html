MAKING THE PERFECT NOODLE

Ever thought about characterizing what the “perfect” noodle looks like? This is the final project for my Control of Manufacturing Processes class. In the project, our team aims to explore the effects of varying key ingredients and cooking parameters on the physical properties of egg noodles using Design of Experiments (DOE) in order to generate an optimal egg noodle recipe.

PROJECT BACKGROUND

Egg noodles are a popular and versatile staple in many cuisines, valued for their unique texture, flavor, and versatility in various dishes. Recipes for egg noodles rely heavily on subjective feel and experience. The composition and preparation of egg noodles play a critical role in determining their final characteristics, such as thickness, color, and elasticity. This experiment aims to explore the effects of varying key ingredients and cooking parameters on the physical properties of egg noodles.

VARIABLES 

The input variables in the experiment include the amount of eggs in egg, the amount of flour, the amount of milk and the cooking time. The center points (0) for each input variable are taken directly from the standard recipe online and we designed the high (+1) and low (-1) points values.   

The first response variable we selected is the % increase in thickness, which can reflect the moisture content in the cooked noodles. The second response variable is the % elongation at break, which can be a measure of the stretchiness. The third one is color, while this may have  little realistic impact, we wanted to examine how the treatments will affect the final color. The final response is the taste, which is a subject measurement done by conduct single blind taste tests.

DESIGN OF EXPERIMENTS 

The experiment was designed as a 2^(4-1) half-factorial experiment with two levels of the four parameters: flour quantity, egg quantity, milk quantity, and cook time. This coupled with a single center point treatment. We chose the defining relationship to be I=ABCD indicating a resolution IV experiment that is balanced in all main factors and orthogonal in all main factors. A half-factorial experiment was chosen to allow the characterization of the four main effects with half of the two-factor interactions while reducing the number of treatments needed.

STANDARDIZATION 

Mixing the ingredients is the first important step in making the pasta dough. Measured quantities of milk, flour, egg, and salt were all thoroughly mixed on the mix setting of a 4.5-quart KitchenAid stand mixer. When the doughs are rested, pasta noodles are produced through the KitchenAid Pasta roller attachment. As for the noodles used for the elongation test, we designed a dog-bone punch was used to stamp standardized pasta shapes. The punch was designed in Fusion 360 and 3D printed out of PLA.  The geometry of the dog-bone punch was designed to produce samples following ASTM D-412-A geometry.

DATA ANALYSIS

I used MATLAB to construct regression analysis for all of the response variables. The figures below shows the final model for the % elongation at break with all the insignificant factors removed. At 95% confidence level, the elongation at break is only correlated with x2 (flour), x3 (milk), and x1:x3 (interaction with egg and milk). The reduced model is y = 0.474 + 0.099x2 + 0.083x3 + 0.052x1x3. The relationship between the rest of the response variables and the input variables are examined in a similar manner.

FINAL RESULTS 

M​ After all the models have been generated, I used MATLAB again to visualize the response surface in order to conduct optimization. Results show that for maximum thickness condition, cooking time should be set to high and the interaction between egg and flour should be set to low. For maximum elongation condition, flour milk, and interaction between egg and milk should all be set to high. For color, no significant model has been found. Finally, we concluded that taste is positively related to the amount of milk added.
