FORMULATING & OPTIMIZING FACTORIO

Factorio is a game about building and creating automated factories to produce items of increasing complexity within an infinite 2D world. It is often regarded as one of the most challenging factory-building games. In this project, our team used what we learned about manufacturing systems to formulate the mechanism of the game as well as to develop an optimized strategy to achieve the end-game criteria.

PROJECT BACKGROUND

Factorio is a real-time strategy game developed by Wube Software. Released in 2020, the game quickly gained popularity for its unique blend of resource management, automation, and intricate logistics. In Factorio, players find themselves crash-landed on an alien planet and must build industrial infrastructure to eventually construct a rocket and leave the planet. 

Our group hope to use what we learned about manufacturing systems to plan an optimized factory in the video game Factorio capable of launching 1 rocket per minute, (the end-game criteria). The game “officially” ends the first time you launch a rocket which often takes an experienced player 50-100 hours to complete. We hope to optimize a factory that can do that every minute. At the fundamental level, the team is trying to see if the game can be formulated and optimized using the principles and knowledge such as Little’s Law, simulation, and optimization.

MATERIAL FLOW MAPPING

We started off by mapping the recipes and the material flows of all the products in the game. At the beginning of the game, the player is thrown into a randomly generated map with randomly distributed raw materials including coal, iron, copper and crude oil. The player must start with the raw materials to build their factory, which contains several facilities with intertwined relationships. Finally, the end goal of the game is to launch the rocket and escape the planet. 

After initial game playing, we collected data such as the processing time at different facilities and the recipes for all the intermediate products. Then, we worked backward from the end product, which is the rocket, and broke down each intermediate product step by step until we reached the raw materials including iron ore, copper ore, coal, and crude oil. We recorded the data in a spreadsheet to track how many intermediate products and raw materials are required to produce 1 rocket part (100 rocket parts make 1 rocket).  

CAPACITY PLANNING

With all the data collected, I visualized all the relationships between products on a node-based network system and conducted capacity planning. To determine the values for each production node, we worked backward from the rocket to the raw materials using knowledge of the recipes and cycle times to determine the requisite rates. According to the ratios, to maintain a production rate of 0.0167 of the rocket/s, we need to supply low-density structure, rocket fuel, and rocket computer at a rate of 20 parts per second. We completed the rest of this flow chart similarly until all the production nodes had been filled. We then created our first model aimed at predicting the number of facilities needed at each node. The model incorporates the recipes and cycle times to calculate the requisite number of machines operating to achieve a given production demand using Little’s Law.

OPTIMIZATION OF MATERIAL FLOW

The oil refining process is the most complex part of the entire gaming mechanism and our initial model failed to capture the dynamic relationships within. The figure below shows the isolated oil refining process. The upstream facilities demand three outputs from this process, which are petroleum gas, light oil and solid fuel. Crude oil goes into oil processing facilities, and three products, petroleum gas, light oil and heavy oil, come out. The default process further uses petroleum gas to produce solid fuel and completely discards the heavy oil, which does not contribute directly to the production of the rocket but could be potentially broken down to make other useful products. 

In fact, the complete oil refining process flow is shown in the figure below. The relationship is a lot more complex than our previous model. Therefore, our goal is to determine which recipe to choose, and how many materials to send through this network, in order to minimize the total usage of the raw material, the crude oil.

OPTIMIZATION FORMULATION

This problem can be analyzed as a network optimization problem. Below is my mathematical formulation for the problem. There are a total of 7 decision variables, each corresponding to a decision of whether to send a product to one location over the other. The objective is to minimize the total usage of crude oil. For the constraints, the first three are the demand constraints for petroleum gas, light oil, and heavy oil respectively, and the rest are for non-negative flows.

Decision Variables

  • a (crude oil sent to basic processing)
  • b (crude oil sent to advanced processing)
  • c (heavy oil sent to heavy oil cracking)
  • d (secondary light oil sent to light oil cracking)
  • e (light oil sent to light oil cracking)
  • f (petroleum gas used to produce solid fuel)
  • g (light oil used to produce solid fuel)

Objective Function

  • Minimize (a+b) (Minimize the total usage of crude oil)

Constraints

  • 0.45a + 0.55b + 0.67(d + e) - f >= 6075 (demand for petroleum gas)
  • 0.75c + d + 0.45b - e - g >=200 (demand for light oil)
  • 0.05f + 0.05g + 0.1(0.25b - c) >= 100 (demand for solid fuel)
  • 0.25b - c >= 0 (non-negative flow)
  • 0.75c - d >= 0 (non-negative flow)
  • 0.45b - e >= 0 (non-negative flow)
  • a, b, c, d, e, f, g >= 0 (non-negative variables)

Optimization results

  • a= 0, b = 7040, c = 1769, d = 120, e = 3168, f = 0, g = 1000

The optimized condition yields a minimum crude oil usage of 7040, whereas in the previous model, the total crude oil usage was 11490. My optimization for the material flow saved 38.7% of the raw material usage.

IMPLEMENTATION

Finally, we implemented our model in the actual game. To exercise our knowledge of the Toyota production system and lean manufacturing, we did our best to organize our final factory to minimize resource transportation. The final factory layout was designed around keeping various cells nearby but distinct. The in-game data we collected from the factory that we designed validated our formulation and successfully achieved our initial objective of launching 1 rocket per minute.
