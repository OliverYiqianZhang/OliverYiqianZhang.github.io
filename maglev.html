MAGLEV MOTION CONTROL SYSTEM

This is my capstone design project for my Bachelor's degree in Mechanical Engineering at Shanghai Jiao Tong University. In the project, I led an interdisciplinary team of 5 people to design and manufacturing a lab-scale maglev linear motion control system as an experimental tool for future students to learn control theory.

PROJECT BACKGROUND

Traditional motor-driven linear motion systems feature excessive vibration, causing a dynamic error in measurements and performance. Maglev technology can solve this problem by eliminating mechanical contact to create a stable, vibration-free, and precisely controlled floating platform. This project aimed to design and build a lab-scale maglev linear motion system for future students in the Mechanical Engineering Lab II course to study control theory. The project was broken down into the mechanical system, the electrical system, and the control system. I was responsible for leading the mechanical design of the prototype.

SYSTEM OVERVIEW

To provide a better context of the project, below is the system overview diagram. The mechanical system design realized the levitation structure using the electromagnetic forces between the electromagnets and the ferromagnets. Hall sensors are installed into the electromagnets to collect data about the position of the mover. The sensor data are transmitted to the control system embedded with the electrical PCB circuit. The PCB circuit sends the digital signal to the upper computer and the users can input control commands through the user interface. The control system then sends the control signal back to the electromagnets through the PCB circuit and thus controlling the motion of the mover.

STATOR DESIGN

The mechanical system Design consists of a fixed stator and a levitated mover. The stator refers to a fixed rail on which the levitated object (mover) will move. The figure below shows the detailed components of the stator. The core of the stator is the three electromagnets that will provide levitation and propulsion for the mover. One electromagnet is placed above the base plate and two below. Hall sensors are installed at the top of every groove to monitor the magnetic field of the electromagnet in real time to detect the current location of the mover.

MOVER DESIGN

The mover refers to the levitated object that can move linearly along the stator. The figure below shows the detailed components of the mover. Corresponding to the three electromagnets on the stator, the mover has three ferromagnet arrays, one placed below the top plate and two below the two bottom plates. The ferromagnet arrays are manufactured by assembling 9 pieces of N54 neodymium magnets to form the Halbach array since it will produce the maximum electromagnetic force. The magnetizing direction of each ferromagnet array is shown in the bottom figure.

VALIDATION

To validate the design, I conducted Finite Element Analysis (FEA) on some key load-bearing components on the stator and the mover. The satisfactory safety factors confirmed the robustness of the structure design. Moreover, to check the feasibility of the design, we also simulated the electromagnetic field between the electromagnet and the ferromagnet using COMSOL Multiphysics.

MANUFACTURING

Due to the high precision requirements of the prototype, most components on the stator and the mover were CNC milled/laser cut 6061 Aluminum. The cores of the electromagnets were also CNC machined out of DT4-C soft iron. We wrapped the coils ourselves using enameled wire. FINAL RESULTS

Since this is a long-term project that requires continuous development, our team only completed the initial development stage of the prototype. Although future improvements and integration are still need, our team were confident that our maglev system will be a valuable asset to the Mechanical Engineering Lab II, providing students with an advanced experimental tool for academic and research purposes.
